Rise to Power

With Mark Antony, and Marcus Aemilius Lepidus, Augustus formed the Second Triumvirate to kill Caesar's assassins at the Battle of Phillipi (42 BC) in the Roman province of Macedonia, near present-day Kavala, Greece. However, the coalition dissolved in 32 BC, which entailed a civil war against Mark Antony and Cleopatra VII of Egypt, culminating the Battle of Actium in 31 BC. From a landslide, Octavian cemented his victory after the battle, which led to their opponents taking their own lives, which marked the de facto conclusion of the Roman Republic.


Reinforcing Justice

Despite the collapse of the Roman Republic, Augustus was able to retain his power in the senate, and was granted sovereignty over Rome. Penultimately, he established the Principate as a scheme to manipulate authority into his hands, naming himself as the princeps. Additionally, he accrued Tribunician power - granting Octavian to propose legislation - and the Greater Proconsular Command, which allowed him to yield military control. Overall, these principles permitted Augustus to rule as Emperor, without claiming the title of King.
